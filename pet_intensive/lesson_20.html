69 Describe an old person who has an interesting life and you enjoy talking to him/her

Part 2 Cue Card:

You should say: Who this person is; Where he/she lives; What his/her life is like; What you like to talk about with him/her; And explain why you enjoy talking to him/her.

Band 7.5+ Sample Response:

"I'd love to tell you about an exceptional elderly gentleman named Mr. Lam, who resides next door to my family's house. He is a retired merchant marine officer in his late seventies. Over his decades-long maritime career, Mr. Lam literally traversed the globe, visiting nearly a hundred countries and collecting fascinating stories. I think he has an incredibly interesting life because his experiences aren't just limited to books; he survived fierce oceanic storms, navigated cultural differences in distant ports, and witnessed global historic transitions. I thoroughly enjoy talking to him because he is an absolute fountain of wisdom. Every weekend, we sit on his porch, and as he pours hot green tea, he reminisces about his voyages. His outlook on life is remarkably positive, and his broad worldviews always challenge my perspectives and offer me valuable life lessons."

Lexical Masterclass:

1. Reminisce (verb) /ˌrem.ɪˈnɪs/ - To enjoy remembering and talking about past events or experiences.

2. Globetrotter (noun) /ˈɡləʊbˌtrɒt.ər/ - A person who travels widely and frequently all over the world.

3. Fountain of wisdom (idiom) - A person who has a vast amount of knowledge, experience, and sound advice.

Part 3 Extensions:

1. Should companies employ older workers?

Ans: Yes, firms should actively recruit older workers. This is because senior employees have decades of accumulated professional experience and industry connections. For example, a senior manager can guide young teams through market crises. Hence, their presence ensures stability.

2. What do you think older people can contribute at work?

Ans: Older workers primarily contribute deep institutional knowledge and mentorship. Their presence ensures that traditional professional standards are maintained. For instance, senior engineers can pass down intricate design methods to junior staff. Therefore, they bridge technical skills gaps.

3. Why do governments make retirement policies?

Ans: Governments enforce retirement policies to regulate the labor market and sustain public pensions. Having a clear retirement threshold allows younger generations to enter the workforce. For example, mandatory retirement frees up entry-level and executive positions. Thus, it prevents structural unemployment.

4. When do you think is the best time to retire?

Ans: The optimal time to retire is when an individual has achieved financial security and is physically ready to slow down. This varies widely, but typically occurs in one's early sixties. For example, retiring at sixty-two allows people to pursue hobbies while still physically healthy. Consequently, it guarantees a higher quality of life.

5. Do you think people should spend more time with their grandparents?

Ans: Absolutely, spending time with grandparents is highly beneficial for a youth's moral development. This is because grandparents pass down oral histories and traditional moral guidelines. For instance, hearing about a grandparent's wartime struggles builds deep resilience in children. Therefore, it strengthens family continuity.

6. Is it beneficial to live with elderly people?

Ans: Yes, multi-generational living offers major emotional and economic benefits. It allows family members to share household duties and reduce childcare costs. For example, grandparents can supervise children while parents work. Thus, it fosters mutual support and domestic stability.

7. How can society better support older people who want to continue working?

Ans: Society should offer flexible, part-time working hours and subsidize digital literacy training for seniors. This is because rapid technological updates often marginalize older professionals. For instance, short courses on computer tools let seniors manage online administration easily. Hence, they remain economically active.

8. What challenges do older workers face in the modern job market?

Ans: Older workers often face age discrimination and struggle to adapt to fast-paced digital tools. Many companies assume seniors cannot learn new software. For example, a veteran secretary might be laid off when a company transitions to automated cloud systems. Consequently, they suffer from technological exclusion.

9. Do you think mandatory retirement ages should be abolished? Why or why not?

Ans: Yes, mandatory retirement should be abolished to protect individual freedom and combat ageism. People's cognitive capacities do not automatically deteriorate at a specific age. For instance, many seventy-year-olds remain highly sharp and capable of contributing. Therefore, ability should dictate employment, not age.

10. How can younger and older generations learn from each other in the workplace?

Ans: They can establish mutual mentorship programs. Younger workers can share insights on digital marketing and AI tools, while older workers can teach negotiation and conflict resolution. For example, a young staff member can teach a senior how to use video tools, while learning management patience. Thus, it builds cooperative progress.

70 Describe a good friend who is important to you

Part 2 Cue Card:

You should say: Who he/she is; How/where you got to know him/her; How long you have known each other; And explain why he/she is important to you.

Band 7.5+ Sample Response:

"I want to share my thoughts on my dearest friend, Huy, who has been an indispensable part of my life for over a decade. We first crossed paths in middle school, sitting next to each other in mathematics class, and we hit it off immediately. Over the years, we've shared countless experiences, from preparing for university entrance exams to backpacking across rustic villages. Huy is incredibly important to me because he is my ultimate confidant and a kindred spirit. No matter what trials I face, he stands by me through thick and thin, offering objective advice and unyielding support. In our hyper-competitive world, finding someone who genuinely celebrates your successes and supports you in your lowest moments is rare. His constant presence acts as an anchor of emotional stability for me."

Lexical Masterclass:

1. Confidant (noun) /ˈkɒn.fɪ.dænt/ - A person with whom one shares a secret or private matter, trusting them not to tell others.

2. Thick and thin (idiom) - Under all circumstances, no matter how difficult or challenging they are.

3. Kindred spirit (noun phrase) - A person whose interests, beliefs, or attitudes are similar to one's own.

Part 3 Extensions:

1. How do children make friends at school?

Ans: Children form bonds naturally through shared play and classroom proximity. This is because proximity facilitates frequent interactions and cooperative learning tasks. For example, working on group science posters or playing tag at recess lets children share immediate joy. Hence, shared play is the primary foundation.

2. How do children make friends when they are not at school?

Ans: Outside of school, children connect through extracurricular classes, neighborhood playgrounds, or family gatherings. These settings offer structural, organized play. For instance, joining a weekend swimming club allows kids to interact with peers from different schools. Therefore, hobbies serve as great social catalysts.

3. Do you think it is better for children to have a few close friends or many casual friends?

Ans: It is far better for children to have a few deep, close friendships. This is because close relationships build emotional resilience, trust, and psychological security. For example, a child with one trusted confidant can share worries openly, whereas many casual friends only offer superficial play. Hence, quality overrides quantity.

4. Do you think a child's relationship with friends can be replaced by that with other people, like parents or other family members?

Ans: No, peer relationships are unique and irreplaceable. While parents provide vital security and guidance, they represent hierarchical figures. Friendships, conversely, are built on egalitarian foundations. For instance, a child learns compromise, negotiation, and modern slang with peers. Thus, peer dynamics are crucial for social maturity.

5. What are the differences between friends made inside and outside the workplace?

Ans: Workplace friendships often involve shared professional goals and corporate politics, whereas external friendships are built purely on shared interests. For example, colleagues might limit conversations to avoid professional conflicts, while childhood friends can be brutally honest. Therefore, external friendships offer deeper emotional sanctuary.

6. Do you think it's possible for bosses and their employees to become friends?

Ans: It is possible but highly difficult to maintain without professional compromises. This is because hierarchical differences require objective evaluation, which conflicts with friendship loyalties. For instance, a boss might struggle to critique a friend’s poor performance. Consequently, it requires strict boundary management.

7. How has social media changed the way people maintain friendships?

Ans: Social media allows people to maintain vast networks easily, but it has diluted emotional depth. This is because direct, face-to-face interactions are replaced by liking photos or sending brief messages. For example, we know a friend's vacation details but miss their actual emotional state. Thus, it trades quality for quantity.

8. Do you think it's more difficult to maintain long-distance friendships now than in the past?

Ans: No, contemporary messaging apps make maintaining connection far easier. However, finding dedicated time for long video calls remains a challenge. For instance, video calling across time zones requires careful scheduling. Consequently, while technology helps, it still demands deliberate effort.

9. What role do cultural differences play in forming international friendships?

Ans: Cultural variations introduce exciting learning curves but can also lead to communication errors. Understanding varying body language, gestures, and levels of directness is essential. For example, a direct German style might initially surprise an indirect Vietnamese speaker. Hence, cross-cultural empathy is vital for bonding.

10. What qualities do you think are most important in a good friend, and why?

Ans: Unwavering loyalty, integrity, and active listening are the most crucial qualities. This is because a true friend must be reliable during crises and offer honest, constructive feedback. For instance, a loyal friend will support you during career setbacks. Therefore, trust is the ultimate cornerstone.

71 Describe a friend of yours who is good at music/singing

Part 2 Cue Card:

You should say: Who this friend is; When/where you listen to his/her music/singing; What kind of music/songs he/she is good at; And explain how you feel when listening to his music/singing.

Band 7.5+ Sample Response:

"I'd love to describe my talented high school classmate, Lan, who possesses an extraordinary gift for acoustic guitar and singing. We met during our school's freshman music club audition, and her performance completely blew me away. Lan is a true virtuoso; she has this uncanny ability to play intricate melodies by ear and holds a pitch-perfect singing voice that instantly captivates any audience. What I find most fascinating is how she can seamlessly blend folk and contemporary acoustic pop. I feel immensely proud and inspired by her musical talent. Whenever we gather for reunions, she brings her guitar and plays timeless acoustic covers. Watching her perform is deeply hypnotic and therapeutic. It's clear she puts her heart and soul into every chord, and I genuinely believe her talent has the potential to move audiences on a much grander stage."

Lexical Masterclass:

1. Virtuoso (noun) /ˌvɜː.tʃuˈəʊ.səʊ/ - A person highly skilled in music or another artistic pursuit.

2. Pitch-perfect (adjective) /ˈpɪtʃˌpɜː.fɪkt/ - Singing or playing in perfect tune; absolutely correct in pitch.

3. Strike a chord (idiom) - To create an emotional response or connect deeply with others.

Part 3 Extensions:

1. What kind of music is popular in your country?

Ans: In Vietnam, V-pop and traditional acoustic folk music are exceptionally popular. This is because younger audiences love catchy, electronic pop, while older demographics prefer nostalgic, slow melodies. For example, V-pop hits dominate YouTube trending charts daily. Hence, modern pop leads the market.

2. What kind of music do young people like?

Ans: Contemporary youth are heavily drawn to indie-pop, hip-hop, and electronic dance music. This is attributable to their desire for energetic, expressive, and relatable lyrics. For instance, hip-hop festivals are packed with university students every weekend. Therefore, modern beats dominate youth playlists.

3. What are the differences between young people's and old people's preferences in music?

Ans: Younger generations favor fast-paced, experimental digital genres, whereas older people prefer slow, acoustic, and traditional vocal arrangements. This is because elders seek relaxing, nostalgic melodies, while youth crave excitement. For example, seniors enjoy Bolero ballads, while youth stream techno. Thus, tempo and style divide preferences.

4. What are the benefits of children learning a musical instrument?

Ans: Learning an instrument enhances a child’s spatial intelligence, fine motor skills, and emotional resilience. Playing music requires coordinated brain activity and intense discipline. For example, practicing the piano regularly improves academic concentration. Hence, it fosters holistic development.

5. Do you know what kind of music children like today?

Ans: Today, children are exposed to catchy, simple, and repetitive nursery songs from digital animated channels. This is because high-energy, animated visual aids make musical tunes highly addictive for toddlers. For instance, viral songs like 'Baby Shark' grab children's attention for hours. Thus, digital nursery pop is their favorite.

6. Do you think the government should invest more money in concerts?

Ans: Yes, public investment in music concerts helps preserve classical music, supports local artists, and enriches public cultural life. Orchestras and traditional folk groups cannot survive purely on ticket sales. For example, subsidizing civic opera houses makes high-art accessible to the general public. Consequently, it preserves cultural heritage.

7. How has streaming technology changed the way people discover new music?

Ans: Streaming algorithms analyze listening histories to recommend niche, independent artists instantly. This has broken the traditional monopoly of major radio stations. For instance, curated playlists on Spotify introduce listeners to global indie bands daily. Hence, it has democratized music exposure.

8. Do you think traditional music should be preserved and promoted more actively?

Ans: Absolutely, traditional music represents a country’s unique cultural soul. Without active promotion, ancient musical forms risk dying out completely as younger generations ignore them. For example, organizing national traditional instruments contests keeps folk arts alive. Thus, active promotion prevents cultural loss.

9. What impact does music have on mental health and emotional wellbeing?

Ans: Music has a highly therapeutic effect, directly lowering cortisol levels and alleviating stress. Listening to slow, harmonic frequencies helps the brain enter relaxing states. For instance, ambient sounds can reduce anxiety in busy commuters. Therefore, it acts as a natural mood elevator.

10. Should music education be mandatory in schools? Why or why not?

Ans: Yes, music education should be compulsory as it fosters creative cognitive pathways and emotional intelligence. Standard academic subjects do not develop aesthetic appreciation or team harmony. For example, school choir classes teach students mutual cooperation. Consequently, it guarantees a balanced education.

72 Describe a great dinner you and your friends or family members enjoyed

Part 2 Cue Card:

You should say: What you had; Who you had the dinner with; What you talked about during the dinner; And explain why you enjoyed it.

Band 7.5+ Sample Response:

"I'd like to share an experience of an exceptionally memorable celebratory dinner we had last autumn to mark my sister's graduation. We dined at a rustic, traditional garden restaurant on the outskirts of Hue. I enjoyed this feast with my immediate family and three of my sister's closest friends. The menu was an absolute culinary masterclass, featuring a vast assortment of local specialties, including mouth-watering roasted duck, steamed sea bass with lemongrass, and beautiful lotus-seed rice. What made this dinner so special was the exquisite atmospheric environment of the venue—open-air garden tables under soft lantern lighting. In our busy, separate routines, we rarely find time to slow down and connect. Sitting together, sharing delicious food, and listening to my sister reminisce about her college struggles created a profound sense of warmth and social cohesion. It was a perfect bonding moment that I'll cherish."

Lexical Masterclass:

1. Culinary (adjective) /ˈkʌl.ɪ.nər.i/ - Of or relating to cooking or the kitchen.

2. Mouth-watering (adjective) /ˈmaʊθˌwɔː.tər.ɪŋ/ - Smelling, looking, or sounding delicious, causing saliva to gather.

3. Break bread (idiom) - To share a meal with someone, often as a sign of friendship, reconciliation, or community.

Part 3 Extensions:

1. Do people prefer to eat out at restaurants or eat at home during the Spring Festival?

Ans: In Vietnam, the majority of people strongly prefer eating home-cooked meals during Tet. This is because Tet is deeply centered on family reunions, ancestral offerings, and cooking traditional dishes together. For example, preparing Banh Chung is a sacred ritual. Thus, dining at home is preferred.

2. What food do you eat on special occasions, like during the Spring Festival or the Mid-autumn Festival?

Ans: On Tet, we consume sticky rice cakes, jellied meat, and pickled onions, while the Mid-Autumn Festival is famous for sweet mooncakes. These foods hold deep symbolic meanings of prosperity and reunion. For instance, circular mooncakes represent family completeness. Hence, they are essential culinary elements.

3. Why do people like to have meals together during important festivals?

Ans: Sharing festive meals strengthens family bonds and reinforces communal ties. In daily life, work schedules isolate family members, making holiday dinners vital checkpoints for re-bonding. For example, having a large family dinner allows members to catch up. Therefore, it fosters social cohesion.

4. Is it a hassle to prepare a meal at home?

Ans: Preparing elaborate meals is highly time-consuming, but simple, everyday cooking is not a major hassle. It requires organized grocery shopping and prep work. For instance, quick stir-fries can be made in under twenty minutes. Thus, with proper planning, it is highly manageable.

5. What do people often talk about during meals?

Ans: Dining conversations generally focus on light, everyday topics like school achievements, career updates, and humor. We avoid highly controversial political topics during meals to keep a pleasant atmosphere. For example, children might share school stories. Hence, it remains a lighthearted exchange.

6. People are spending less and less time having meals with their families these days. Is this good or bad?

Ans: This is a highly negative development, as it weakens family relationships and promotes individual isolation. Missing regular family dinners means parents miss early warnings about their children's stress. For instance, teens who eat alone show higher rates of depression. Consequently, it harms domestic harmony.

7. How have eating habits changed in your country over the past few decades?

Ans: Urban diets have shifted from traditional, fresh, home-cooked food toward convenient fast food and delivery services. This is attributable to fast-paced lifestyles and the rise of food apps. For example, office workers now order bubble tea and pizzas daily. Hence, convenience dominates modern diets.

8. Do you think fast food is affecting traditional family meals?

Ans: Yes, the ubiquity of fast food has significantly reduced the frequency of communal family dining. Because individual members can grab quick burgers independently, the ritual of cooking and sitting together is lost. For instance, kids often eat fast food in their rooms. Therefore, it disrupts domestic routines.

9. What role do meals play in building family relationships?

Ans: Communal dining acts as a vital communicative hub where family members share support and build deep empathy. The act of sharing food fosters a safe space for open conversations. For example, children feel secure sharing their worries over dinner. Consequently, meals are critical bonding blocks.

10. Should parents teach their children how to cook traditional dishes?

Ans: Yes, teaching culinary arts preserves historic recipes and builds practical self-reliance skills. Knowing how to prepare ancestral dishes keeps cultural values alive. For instance, a student studying abroad can cook traditional soup to combat homesickness. Thus, it guarantees cultural and practical survival.

73 Describe an interesting traditional story

Part 2 Cue Card:

You should say: What the story is about; When/how you knew it; Who told you the story; And explain how you felt when you first heard it.

Band 7.5+ Sample Response:

"I'd like to talk about a highly fascinating traditional Vietnamese legend known as 'The Legend of Saint Giong' or 'Thanh Giong.' I first learned about this story in early childhood, when my grandmother would narrate it as a bedtime tale. The main character is a three-year-old boy who, despite his age, had never spoken or walked. However, when the nation faced a massive foreign invasion, he suddenly stood up, grew into a giant, rode a flaming iron horse, and brandished a sword to single-handedly defeat the invaders. I think this story is deeply interesting because Giong did not fight for personal glory or a royal title; after securing victory, he immediately flew up to the heavens, leaving behind a legacy of absolute selflessness. It beautifully captures the spirit of national unity and heroic patriotism that has defined our cultural history for centuries."

Lexical Masterclass:

1. Narrate (verb) /ˈnær.eɪt/ - To tell a story or describe a series of events.

2. Brandish (verb) /ˈbræn.dɪʃ/ - To wave something (especially a weapon) in a triumphant or excited way.

3. Patriotic (adjective) /ˌpæt.riˈɒt.ɪk/ - Having or showing great love and support for one's country.

Part 3 Extensions:

1. What kind of stories do children like?

Ans: Children are naturally drawn to highly imaginative fairy tales and adventure stories featuring talking animals or magical worlds. This is because magic appeals to their natural curiosity and stimulates creative play. For example, stories like 'Peter Pan' capture their minds instantly. Thus, fantasy is highly favored.

2. What are the benefits of listening to stories before bed?

Ans: Bedtime storytelling relaxes the nervous system, enhances language skills, and fosters strong parent-child relationships. Hearing rhythmic narratives helps the brain release tension and prepare for deep sleep. For instance, vocabulary retention is significantly higher in children who hear bedtime stories. Hence, it guarantees intellectual and emotional health.

3. Why do most children like listening to stories before bedtime?

Ans: It provides them with an immense sense of security and cozy comfort at the end of the day. The physical presence and soothing voice of a parent make children feel safe from nighttime fears. For example, children fall asleep quickly after hearing a happy story. Therefore, it satisfies psychological needs.

4. What can children learn from stories?

Ans: Children absorb vital moral lessons, such as the value of honesty, hard work, and empathy, through character behaviors. Stories simplify complex real-world lessons into understandable plots. For instance, 'The Boy Who Cried Wolf' teaches the severe cost of lying. Thus, stories serve as moral compasses.

5. Do all stories for children have happy endings?

Ans: Not all, but the vast majority of stories incorporate happy resolutions to reinforce optimism and moral justice. A happy ending suggests that goodness ultimately triumphs over evil, which is reassuring for children. For example, Cinderella escaping her stepmother rewards her patience. Hence, positive endings are vital for encouraging hope.

6. Is a good storyline important for a movie?

Ans: Absolutely, a robust, logical plot is the fundamental core of any memorable film. High-budget special effects cannot salvage a movie with a weak or incoherent storyline. For example, classic films with simple graphics but intense plots still captivate audiences decades later. Consequently, narrative structure is paramount.

7. How do traditional stories help preserve cultural values?

Ans: Traditional tales carry historic records of national heroism, filial piety, and community codes of conduct. By passing these down, nations preserve their unique spiritual heritage against modern homogenization. For instance, the legend of Saint Giong instills deep patriotism in Vietnamese children. Therefore, stories preserve national identity.

8. Do you think digital storytelling is replacing traditional oral storytelling?

Ans: Yes, interactive videos, apps, and audiobooks have largely overtaken face-to-face oral narration. This is attributable to the sheer convenience and addictive visual stimuli of modern screens. For example, parents often play YouTube stories for children instead of narrating them themselves. Hence, digital media dominates storytelling today.

9. What makes a story memorable and timeless?

Ans: Timeless stories incorporate universal human themes, such as love, betrayal, sacrifice, and growth, which remain relevant across centuries and cultures. These themes touch deep emotional chords in every generation. For example, Shakespeare's plots are still adapted globally. Thus, emotional truth makes stories immortal.

10. Should schools include more traditional stories in their curriculum?

Ans: Yes, integrating local folk literature into academic schedules strengthens cultural literacy and creative thinking. Textbooks focus heavily on math and science, neglecting literary heritage. For instance, reading ancient epics teaches children historical literary devices. Consequently, it guarantees a balanced, culturally rooted education.

74 Describe a kind of foreign food you like and you have had

Part 2 Cue Card:

You should say: What the food is; When and where you had it; Why you like it; And explain how you feel about it.

Band 7.5+ Sample Response:

"I would love to share my passion for authentic Japanese Ramen, particularly Tonkotsu Ramen, which I first tasted at a small, cozy noodle bar in Ho Chi Minh City about a year ago. It has since become a monthly culinary treat for me. This dish consists of wheat noodles served in a rich, velvety pork bone broth that has been simmered for hours, topped with tender chashu pork, a soft-boiled egg, and seaweed. What makes this foreign food so enjoyable is the striking complexity of its flavors. The broth has an intensely savory, umami profile that completely blankets the palate. Eating a steaming bowl of ramen is not just a basic meal; it is a highly therapeutic and comforting experience, especially during the rainy season. It represents a beautiful culinary masterpiece where every single ingredient has been prepared with extreme precision."

Lexical Masterclass:

1. Velvety (adjective) /ˈvel.və.ti/ - Having a smooth, soft, and luxurious texture or flavor.

2. Umami (noun/adjective) /uːˈmɑː.mi/ - A category of taste in food corresponding to the flavor of glutamates (savory/meaty).

3. Suit one's palate (idiom) - To be highly agreeable, delicious, or satisfying to someone's personal taste.

Part 3 Extensions:

1. Do most people like to eat the food they are familiar with or try new food?

Ans: Generally, children and older adults prefer familiar traditional dishes, while young metropolitan residents actively seek novel culinary experiences. This is because experimental dining matches the exploratory lifestyle of youths. For example, teens queue for hours to try newly imported Korean foods. Thus, age dictates choices.

2. What kinds of foreign food are popular in your country?

Ans: Japanese sushi, Korean barbecue, and Italian pizza are highly popular in major Vietnamese cities. This is attributable to their aesthetic presentation and robust flavor profiles that suit our palates. For instance, Korean barbecue spots are packed with families every weekend. Hence, East Asian cuisines lead the market.

3. Do you like to cook at home?

Ans: Yes, cooking at home allows me to control ingredients, manage nutrition, and find a creative, relaxing outlet. Preparing fresh vegetables and simmering broths helps me decompress after long study hours. For example, I cook fresh fish on weekends. Therefore, it is highly therapeutic.

4. Is it expensive to eat out in your country?

Ans: Dining out can range from incredibly cheap at local street food stalls to highly expensive at high-end hotel restaurants. Street food remains highly affordable for daily commuters, while fine dining demands a premium budget. For instance, a bowl of Pho costs under two dollars, whereas high-end sushi costs fifty. Thus, it varies by venue.

5. What's the difference between Vietnamese food and Western food?

Ans: Vietnamese cuisine prioritizes fresh herbs, light broths, and fish sauce, whereas Western food relies heavily on dairy, thick sauces, and wheat-based carbs. This is because our traditional cooking highlights aromatic freshness, while Western cooking focuses on rich, hearty textures. For example, a light noodle soup contrasts sharply with a creamy pasta. Hence, ingredients and textures differ.

6. How has the globalization of cuisine affected local culinary traditions in urban areas?

Ans: It has acted as a double-edged sword by introducing culinary diversity but also threatening the survival of traditional street vendors. High-margin franchise restaurants dominate commercial spaces, pushing out low-cost local eateries. For example, shopping malls are packed with foreign brands instead of local noodle stalls. Consequently, urban gastronomy risks losing its local soul.

7. Do you think traditional foods are healthier than modern fast foods?

Ans: Yes, traditional dishes rely on unprocessed vegetables, steamed proteins, and herbal broths, whereas modern fast food is packed with refined sugar, trans fats, and sodium. Regular fast food consumption leads directly to metabolic illnesses. For example, Pho provides balanced, lean nutrition, while burgers offer empty calories. Thus, traditional diets are superior.

8. How can restaurants promote local, healthy dishes to younger customers?

Ans: They can modernize traditional recipes with beautiful, aesthetic presentations and run visual campaigns on social media. Younger diners eat with their eyes first and value photogenic spaces. For instance, serving local herbal teas in minimalist, trendy cups attracts Gen Z instantly. Therefore, artistic branding is key.

9. Should schools teach students about different international cuisines?

Ans: Yes, culinary education encourages cultural open-mindedness and breaks down social prejudices in children. Understanding how different societies prepare food builds early global literacy. For example, school workshops about Japanese food teach children about sushi etiquette. Hence, it develops well-rounded global views.

10. How do cooking shows and social media influence people's interest in foreign food?

Ans: They leverage highly edited, mouth-watering visuals and step-by-step reels to demystify complex international cooking. Watching viral vlogs makes cooking foreign food seem highly accessible. For instance, short TikTok recipes have inspired millions to cook Italian pasta at home. Thus, media actively drives culinary curiosity.
