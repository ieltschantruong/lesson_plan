Task 1 Line Graph

"The graph below shows the changes of four types of ways to communicate according to different age groups in the UK in 2011. Summarize the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant."
Brainstormed Ideas & Structure
1. Introduction

Paraphrase the prompt: The line graph contrasts the percentages of UK residents in 2011 utilizing four distinct communication channels (post, telephone, mobile phone, and internet) across four age brackets (under 18, 18-35, 36-60, and 60+).

2. Overview

State major trends: Overall, digital and modern channels (internet) drop continuously with age, whereas older populations heavily favor traditional methods (post and telephone). Strikingly, mobile phone usage remains entirely flat at 30% for all demographics under 60 before dropping for seniors.

3. Body Paragraph 1: Traditional Media (Post & Telephone) - Growing Trends
  • Telephone: Starts at 16% for under 18s, climbs to 20% (18-35), rises to 35% (36-60), and peaks at 46% for seniors (60+).
  • Post: Follows a very similar upward trajectory. Commences as the least favored method for under 18s at only 5%, rising steadily to 10% (18-35), 20% (36-60), and peaks at 35% for the oldest bracket.
4. Body Paragraph 2: Modern Media (Internet & Mobile) - Declining/Flat Trends
  • Internet: Plummets drastically. Starts as the outright favorite for under 18s at 50%, decreases to 40% (18-35), drops sharply to ~13% (36-60), and bottoms out at a mere 4% for those over 60.
  • Mobile Phone: Remarkably constant. Stays exactly at 30% for the first three age brackets (under 18, 18-35, and 36-60) before dropping by half to 15% for the 60+ cohort.
Sample Band 8.0 Response

The line graph compares the usage percentages of four communication channels—post, telephone, mobile phone, and the internet—across four different age cohorts in the United Kingdom in 2011.

Overall, there was a stark division in communication preferences based on age. Younger demographics relied heavily on the internet, which declined dramatically in popularity among older cohorts. Conversely, traditional methods like post and telephone displayed a strong, positive correlation with age. Remarkably, mobile phone usage remained completely stable across all age groups under 60 years old.

With respect to traditional communication methods, both telephone and post saw progressive growth as the target audience aged. Telephone usage started at 16% among individuals under 18, grew marginally to 20% for those aged 18–35, reached 35% for the 36–60 age group, and peaked as the most popular medium for seniors aged 60 and over at 46%. Post followed an identical upward trend, starting as the least popular method for under-18s at just 5%, rising steadily to 10% and 20% in consecutive cohorts, and culminating at 35% for the oldest demographic.

In contrast, internet usage dropped significantly with age. It was the most dominant channel for the youngest cohort (under 18) at 50%, dropped slightly to 40% for those aged 18–35, plummeted to roughly 13% for middle-aged adults, and bottomed out at just 4% for the elderly. Meanwhile, mobile phone communication was incredibly stable, maintaining an identical rate of exactly 30% for all age categories under 60, before halving to 15% for the over-60 demographic.

Task 2 Essay

"Some people think that all lawbreakers should be sent to prison, while others think that there are better alternatives for those whose crimes are minor, such as making them do work for the local community. Discuss both views and give your own opinion."
Brainstormed Ideas & Structure
1. Introduction

Introduce the debate: Universal imprisonment vs. community-based alternatives for minor offenders. State opinion: Disagree with universal sentencing; support community service for petty criminals as it aids rehabilitation and cuts government spending.

2. Body Paragraph 1: Why some argue for universal imprisonment
  • Deterrence: A strict, unwavering policy discourages prospective lawbreakers.
  • Public Safety & Retribution: Satisfies the community's demand for justice and ensures that all offenders face physical punishment.
3. Body Paragraph 2: Why others support alternatives (and my opinion)
  • Avoids Criminal Hardening: Throwing petty offenders into cells with hardened criminals often leads to further radicalization or integration into gang circles.
  • Economic Efficiency: Jail maintenance costs taxpayers heavily, whereas community service generates free public works (e.g., painting facilities, cleaning parks).
  • Rehabilitation: Encourages positive social reintegration and cultivates a sense of responsibility toward the community.
4. Conclusion

Summarize both viewpoints. Conclude that a dual-tier justice system (heavy prison sentences for severe crimes, community-focused rehabilitation for minor offenses) is far more productive and economical.

Sample Band 8.0 Response

Opinions differ on whether prison sentences should be applied universally to all lawbreakers or if alternative punishments, such as community service, should be introduced for minor offenses. While universal incarceration is seen by some as a strong deterrent, I believe that rehabilitation-oriented alternatives for non-violent, minor offenders offer a far more productive approach for society.

On the one hand, proponents of sending all lawbreakers to prison believe that a strict, uncompromising legal system is necessary to maintain order. Imprisoning every offender, regardless of the severity of their crime, sends a clear and powerful message to the public, establishing a robust deterrent against unlawful behavior. Furthermore, this approach satisfies the public demand for retribution and ensures that criminals, whether shoplifters or serious felons, are physically removed from society to face immediate and noticeable consequences for their actions.

On the other hand, others advocate for sentencing alternatives for petty crimes, an opinion I strongly support. The primary disadvantage of universal imprisonment is that minor offenders are placed in the same environment as hardened criminals, which often leads to bad influences and a higher likelihood of reoffending. By contrast, alternatives like community work allow petty criminals to pay their debt to society while avoiding the negative influences of jail. Additionally, holding inmates in prison places a massive financial burden on government budgets. Introducing community service programs—such as cleaning public parks, removing graffiti, or assisting in public facilities—saves public funds and actively contributes to local welfare.

In conclusion, while incarceration remains critical for serious, violent crimes to guarantee public safety, applying it universally to minor offenders is counterproductive and expensive. I believe that integrating community service for petty criminals is a far more effective strategy, as it encourages rehabilitation, prevents the escalation of criminal behavior, and economically benefits the local community.
