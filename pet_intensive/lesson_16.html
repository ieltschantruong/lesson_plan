Task 1 Map Comparison

"The map below shows the changes that have taken place at a zoo in Australia between 1960 and now. Summarize the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant."
Brainstormed Ideas & Structure
1. Introduction

Paraphrase the prompt: The two maps illustrate structural modifications made to an Australian zoo from 1960 to the present day.

2. Overview

Identify major structural modifications: Overall, the zoo has modernized its layout. Key updates include the reorganization of animal enclosures (African animals moved and birds/chimps replaced), the introduction of ferry access and an additional northern entrance, the expansion of the gift shop, and the installation of a cable car system.

3. Body Paragraph 1 (Western & Northern Zoo Sectors)
  • Northwest Enclosure: "African animals" (1960) has been replaced with "Wild animals".
  • Southwest Enclosure: "Australian animals" remains in its original position.
  • Northern Boundary: A new entrance and ferry station have been constructed along the harbor, facilitating water transport.
4. Body Paragraph 2 (Central, Southern & Eastern Sectors)
  • Center: "African animals" now resides in the center, next to the "seal show" (which replaced the old bird enclosure). The "chimpanzees" enclosure has been removed.
  • Gift Shop: The southern gift shop has been completely rebuilt into a much larger, multi-story facility.
  • Transit (Cable Car): A new cable car transit line runs diagonally from the new gift shop area to the northeastern picnic zone.
Sample Band 8.0 Response

The two maps illustrate the transformation of an Australian zoo from 1960 to the present day.

Overall, the zoo has undergone extensive modernization, characterized by reorganized animal enclosures, upgraded transportation links, and expanded visitor facilities. Notably, the introduction of water-based transit, an additional entrance, and a cable car system have significantly enhanced accessibility and navigation throughout the park.

In the western half of the zoo, the southwestern "Australian animals" enclosure has remained unchanged. In contrast, the northwestern area, which housed "African animals" in 1960, has been converted into a "Wild animals" enclosure. At the northern boundary, the harbor now features a ferry terminal and a secondary entrance gate, allowing direct access for marine commuters. Meanwhile, the "African animals" exhibit has been relocated to the center-south of the zoo, adjacent to the "seal show," which occupies the former "birds" enclosure. The central "chimpanzees" exhibit has been entirely cleared.

In the southern sector, the original gift shop has been demolished and replaced by a substantially larger, modern facility. From this new gift shop, a diagonal cable car line has been installed, connecting the south-central area directly to the picnic tables in the northeast. The picnic area itself has been preserved, though additional trees have been planted nearby to provide shade.

Task 2 Essay

"Some people think paying taxes is a big enough contribution to their society, while others think people have more responsibilities as members of society than only paying taxes. Discuss both views and give your own opinion."
Brainstormed Ideas & Structure
1. Introduction

State debate: Is paying taxes a citizen's sole societal obligation, or do they have broader moral responsibilities? Thesis: Taxes are crucial, but active civic engagement is vital for a functional, moral society.

2. Body Paragraph 1: Why taxes are considered sufficient
  • Economic Backbone: Taxes fund healthcare, infrastructure, education, and defense.
  • Professional Division of Labor: Paying taxes allows the state to hire professional police, teachers, and sanitation workers, freeing citizens to focus on their private careers.
3. Body Paragraph 2: Why active civic duties are necessary (My Opinion)
  • Social Cohesion: Community volunteering, caring for vulnerable populations, and local cleanup groups solve immediate local issues that government budgets cannot directly address.
  • Moral & Civic Responsibility: A passive society that only pays taxes becomes highly individualistic. Active participation builds empathy, community trust, and a cleaner environment.
4. Conclusion

Reiterate: Taxes keep the state's physical engine running, but active civic involvement is the soul that keeps communities empathetic and cohesive.

Sample Band 8.0 Response

Opinions are divided on whether paying taxes constitutes a citizen's entire duty to their nation, or if being a member of a society carries broader social responsibilities. While taxes provide the essential financial foundation for public services, I believe that active civic participation is indispensable for a healthy and compassionate society.

On the one hand, many argue that paying taxes is the most substantial and sufficient contribution a person can make to their country. Tax revenues serve as the financial backbone of the state, directly funding critical public infrastructure, healthcare systems, schools, and law enforcement. By fulfilling this legal obligation, citizens provide the government with the necessary resources to manage society professionally. Proponents of this view suggest that since taxpayers work to support their households and pay substantial income taxes, they should be free to focus on their private careers, leaving public management to government bodies.

On the other hand, a purely transactional relationship with the state fails to address the socio-emotional needs of a community. Active civic responsibilities, such as volunteering at local shelters, assisting the elderly, or participating in neighborhood cleanup drives, foster social cohesion and mutual trust. Governments, regardless of their budget size, cannot solve every hyper-local issue or eliminate loneliness and neglect. Furthermore, a society where individuals only pay taxes and ignore their neighbors becomes individualistic and disconnected. Caring for the environment, supporting local charity drives, and engaging in constructive local debates are moral duties that cultivate empathy and keep a community together.

In conclusion, while paying taxes is a vital legal duty that keeps the state's physical infrastructure functioning, it represents only the bare minimum of civic obligation. Truly prosperous and cohesive societies rely on the active, moral, and volunteer contributions of citizens who understand that membership in a community requires empathy and action beyond a financial transaction.
