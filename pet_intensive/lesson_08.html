Task 1 Line Graph

"The graph below shows the percentage of people visiting a museum at least once a year between 1980 and 2010. Summarize the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant."
Brainstormed Ideas & Structure
1. Introduction

Paraphrase the prompt: The line chart illustrates the proportion of annual visitors to a museum across four age groups (15-24, 25-44, 45-64, and 65+) over a thirty-year span starting in 1980.

2. Overview

State major trends: Overall museum attendance grew for almost all demographics, with the oldest category (65+) seeing the most significant expansion. Conversely, the youngest age group (15-24) showed minor fluctuations and remained the lowest visiting group.

3. Body Paragraph 1: Older Cohorts (45-64 & 65+)
  • 65+ Group: Extremely sharp increase. Started lowest among older groups in 1980 (~15%) and rose steadily to exceed all others by 2010 (~38%).
  • 45-64 Group: Moderate, continuous growth from about 22% in 1980 to roughly 33% in 2010.
4. Body Paragraph 2: Younger Cohorts (15-24 & 25-44)
  • 25-44 Group: Showed progressive rise, starting near 25% and finishing near 32% with a slight dip in the 1990s.
  • 15-24 Group: Remained stagnant and low. Fluctuated tightly between 10% and 14% over the entire three decades.
Sample Band 7.5+ Response

The line graph compares the percentage of individuals from four different age demographics (15-24, 25-44, 45-64, and over 65) who visited a museum at least once a year between 1980 and 2010.

Overall, museum attendance grew across almost all demographics over the thirty-year timeframe. The most notable expansion was observed in the senior group (aged 65 and over), whereas the youngest age bracket (15-24) showed minor fluctuations and remained the demographic least likely to visit museums.

In 1980, the 25-44 cohort was the most active visiting demographic, with roughly 25% visiting a museum annually, slightly higher than the 45-64 cohort at around 22%. By 2010, both categories experienced steady upwards trajectories, with the 45-64 cohort rising to 33%, while those aged 25-44 rose moderately to finish at approximately 32% despite a brief plateuing phase during the mid-1990s.

Conversely, those aged 65 and above started as a low-visiting category in 1980 at just 15%. However, this senior demographic experienced a massive, uninterrupted surge, culminating at around 38% in 2010 and surpassing all other cohorts to become the leading museum-going demographic. In stark contrast, museum attendance among youth (aged 15-24) remained exceptionally stagnant, starting at roughly 10%, peaking temporarily in 1990 at 14%, and reverting to its initial 10% by the end of the period.

Task 2 Essay

"Extreme sports such as skydiving and skiing are very dangerous and should be banned. To what extent do you agree or disagree with this view?"
Brainstormed Ideas & Structure
1. Introduction

State hook and prompt: The debate on prohibiting high-risk outdoor activities. Express clear position: Strongly disagree with a complete ban, supporting instead regulatory safety parameters and individual freedom of choice.

2. Body Paragraph 1: Why some argue for a ban (Counter-arguments)
  • High risk of fatality: Inherent structural dangers in sports like base jumping or extreme skiing.
  • Economic burden on taxpayers: Expensive search, rescue, and emergency medical response operations.
3. Body Paragraph 2: Arguments against a ban (Our Core Stance)
  • Individual Liberty: Competent adults should have the autonomy to calculate risk and make choices.
  • Regulation over prohibition: Strict rules, specialized gear, and certifications minimize casualties (e.g. licensing requirements for skydivers).
  • Economic and tourist advantages: Extreme sports generate substantial revenue and create thousands of jobs globally.
4. Conclusion

Summarize both parts. Reiterate that strict regulatory frameworks, certified safety guides, and licensing represent a far more balanced solution than an outright ban.

Sample Band 8.0 Response

With the growing popularity of thrill-seeking activities, some argue that extreme sports like skydiving and skiing are too dangerous and should be outlawed. Although the risks associated with these pursuits are undeniably higher than those of typical sports, I strongly disagree with the idea of banning them. Instead, governments should focus on establishing strict safety regulations and requirements.

Admittedly, there are valid safety and societal concerns driving the push for a prohibition. The primary argument revolves around the severe consequences of accidents, which can lead to life-altering injuries or fatalities. Furthermore, extreme sports require substantial public resources when things go wrong. Search and rescue missions, particularly in mountainous terrains for missing skiers, place a heavy financial burden on local taxpayers and can put rescue teams in harm's way. Thus, opponents believe that preventing individuals from engaging in such hazardous sports is necessary for public welfare.

However, an outright ban is an excessively restrictive measure that violates personal freedom. Adult citizens should have the autonomy to calculate danger and engage in recreational activities of their choosing, provided they do not harm others. More importantly, experience has shown that regulation, rather than prohibition, is highly effective. In skydiving, for instance, equipment is subject to strict inspections, and jumps can only be conducted under the supervision of certified instructors. Establishing compulsory licensing, requiring medical clearances, and limiting activities to specific authorized areas can minimize safety hazards while keeping extreme sports legal and accessible.

Additionally, extreme sports have significant economic benefits that should not be overlooked. They represent a thriving industry that fuels global tourism, equipment manufacturing, and local employment. Places like Queenstown in New Zealand or the Swiss Alps rely heavily on extreme sports tourism to sustain their local economies. Banning these sports would result in severe job losses and financial distress for communities centered around outdoor recreation.

In conclusion, while extreme sports involve clear physical dangers and public rescue costs, banning them is not a justified response. Implementing comprehensive safety regulations, mandating high-quality protective gear, and enforcing training licenses offer a far more balanced approach to managing risk while preserving personal liberty and economic vitality.
