01 Describe a long journey you had and would like to take again

Part 2 Cue Card Bullets:

You should say: Where you went; How you traveled; What you did on the journey; And explain why you would like to go on this journey again.

Band 7.5+ Sample Response (Monologue):

"I would like to reminisce about an extraordinarily long and breathtaking journey that I took last year along the Ha Giang Loop in northern Vietnam. I traveled with two of my closest friends, and we decided to do the loop on motorbikes, which we rented locally. This gave us the absolute autonomy to traverse the rugged, winding mountain passes at our own pace and pull over whenever we encountered a particularly awe-inspiring view. On our way, we rode through vast pine forests, stopped at rustic local villages, and stayed at family homestays where we ate traditional food. What made the trip so memorable was the breathtaking scenic beauty of the towering limestone karsts and deep, verdant valleys. I would leap at the chance to embark on this journey again because modern life can be extremely fast-paced and overwhelming. Being on the open road with no distractions was incredibly therapeutic; it allowed me to disconnect completely from my academic responsibilities and fully recharge my batteries. Going back would allow me to experience that profound peace of mind once more."

Lexical Masterclass (Detailed Vocab & Idioms):

1. Traverse (verb) /trəˈvɜːs/

Definition: To travel across or through a path or region.
Collocations: Traverse rugged terrain, traverse mountain passes.
Example: It takes extreme focus to traverse the narrow, winding pathways of the highlands.

2. Therapeutic (adjective) /ˌθerəˈpjuːtɪk/

Definition: Having a healing, relaxing, or beneficial effect on the body or mind.
Collocations: Highly therapeutic effect, therapeutic environment.
Example: Sitting quietly by the river after a long ride was incredibly therapeutic.

3. Off the beaten track (idiom)

Definition: Located in a remote area, far away from where most people live or travel.
Example: We deliberately chose to visit homestays that were situated far off the beaten track.

Part 3 Analytical Extension:

Q: Why do some people prefer long-distance journeys over quick vacations?
Ans (OREO): From my perspective, long-distance journeys are preferred because they facilitate a deeper, more organic connection with the destination. This is due to the fact that quick holidays are often rushed and commercialized, leaving little time for authentic exploration. For example, a traveler driving across a country interacts with diverse rural cultures, whereas a resort tourist remains in a highly curated bubble. Therefore, long-distance travel is fundamentally a transformative experience rather than mere superficial relaxation.

02 Describe a person you know who enjoys working for a family business

Part 2 Cue Card Bullets:

You should say: Who this person is; What family business they work for; What their daily responsibilities are; And explain why you think they enjoy working there.

Band 7.5+ Sample Response (Monologue):

"I'm going to talk about my older cousin, Minh, who is incredibly passionate about working for his family’s traditional lacquerware workshop in Binh Duong province. This business is a key element of our family's heritage, having been passed down through three consecutive generations. Minh's primary responsibilities include managing raw material logistics, supervising the younger artisans, and spearheading their digital marketing campaigns to reach international buyers. Minh absolutely loves his job because it is his primary source of livelihood and gives him a deep sense of purpose. He prefers this over climbing a corporate ladder because he enjoys the flat organizational structure where he can call the shots alongside his father. Furthermore, he feels that he is directly preserving a vanishing craft. He puts his heart and soul into every piece, finding immense joy in seeing his family's legacy thrive in the modern era."

Lexical Masterclass (Detailed Vocab & Idioms):

1. Livelihood (noun) /ˈlaɪvlihʊd/

Definition: The means of securing the necessities of life, such as money to pay for food and housing.
Collocations: Secure a livelihood, sole source of livelihood.
Example: For many villagers, weaving is not just a hobby but their primary livelihood.

2. Call the shots (idiom)

Definition: To make the important decisions; to be in control of a situation.
Example: Since Minh became a co-director, he has the authority to call the shots on overseas trade.

3. Put one's heart and soul into (idiom)

Definition: To do something with maximum effort, energy, and complete dedication.
Example: Minh put his heart and soul into restoring the workshop's historic furnace.

Part 3 Analytical Extension:

Q: What are the main advantages and disadvantages of working in a family business?
Ans (OREO): In my view, the paramount benefit of a family enterprise is the unparalleled level of mutual trust and shared long-term vision. This is because family members are typically motivated by pride and legacy rather than short-term financial gains, which creates a highly supportive workspace. However, the prominent drawback is the blurring of personal and professional boundaries, which can lead to severe domestic arguments. For instance, a disagreement over business budgets during dinner can easily strain family relationships. Therefore, while family businesses offer incredible stability, they require strict boundary management to avoid internal friction.

03 Describe a group activity you took part in

Part 2 Cue Card Bullets:

You should say: What the activity was; When and where you did it; Who you did it with; And explain what you contributed and how you felt about it.

Band 7.5+ Sample Response (Monologue):

"I'd like to describe an extremely rewarding group activity I participated in last autumn. It was a local charity flood-relief drive organized by our neighborhood youth center in Da Nang. Our objective was to collect, organize, and package dry rations, warm clothes, and clean water to distribute to communities affected by a tropical storm. I worked alongside a team of around fifteen other student volunteers. We set up our operations in the local community hall and worked tirelessly for three days straight. My specific role was to manage the inventory and coordinate the loading logistics for the transport vehicles. The team spirit was absolutely contagious; everyone pulled their weight and worked hand-in-hand to hit our targets. As the saying goes, 'many hands make light work,' and we managed to pack over five hundred relief boxes in record time. Completing this task gave me a profound sense of accomplishment and reinforced the importance of social cohesion during crises."

Lexical Masterclass (Detailed Vocab & Idioms):

1. Collaborate (verb) /kəˈlæbəreɪt/

Definition: To work jointly on an activity or project, especially to produce or create something.
Collocations: Collaborate closely, collaborate on a project.
Example: Team members must collaborate effectively to achieve the desired outcome.

2. Pull one's weight (idiom)

Definition: To do one's fair share of work or effort in a collective task.
Example: In our volunteer team, everyone pulled their weight, so no one felt overwhelmed.

3. Many hands make light work (proverb)

Definition: Large tasks are completed quickly and easily when many people work together.
Example: Packing those boxes seemed daunting, but many hands make light work.

Part 3 Analytical Extension:

Q: Is it better for children to study in groups or individually?
Ans (OREO): In my estimation, studying in groups is vastly superior for a child's holistic development. This is because group tasks cultivate vital interpersonal skills, such as active listening, compromise, and empathy, which cannot be developed in isolation. For instance, children working on a science project together must divide roles and solve conflicts constructively. Therefore, while solitary study is effective for memorization, group study is far more effective for preparing children for real-world collaboration.

04 Describe one of your grandparents' job

Part 2 Cue Card Bullets:

You should say: What your grandparent's job was; Where they worked; What their daily tasks were; And explain how you felt about their job.

Band 7.5+ Sample Response (Monologue):

"I would like to describe the noble job of my grandfather, who worked as a dedicated rural country doctor in a remote agricultural village in Ha Tinh province. He practiced medicine during a time of immense economic hardship, when basic clinics and surgical tools were incredibly scarce. His daily responsibilities were vast; he had to treat everything from minor agricultural injuries to severe seasonal illnesses, often walking miles on foot to visit bedridden patients. To my grandfather, medicine was not merely a profession, but a true vocation driven by pure altruism. He frequently treated impoverished families free of charge, accepting fresh produce or simple hand-made gifts as tokens of appreciation instead. I have always looked up to him for his absolute devotion and integrity. His presence made a world of difference in that village, and hearing the locals speak of him with such profound respect makes me incredibly proud of his legacy."

Lexical Masterclass (Detailed Vocab & Idioms):

1. Vocation (noun) /vəʊˈkeɪʃn/

Definition: A strong feeling of suitability for a particular career or occupation; a calling.
Collocations: A true vocation, follow one's vocation.
Example: Nursing is more than a job; it is a vocation that demands deep patience.

2. Look up to (phrasal verb)

Definition: To respect, admire, and view someone as a role model.
Example: I have always looked up to my grandfather for his selflessness and strong principles.

3. Make a world of difference (idiom)

Definition: To have a very major, positive effect on a situation or person.
Example: Planting trees around the local school has made a world of difference to the temperature.

Part 3 Analytical Extension:

Q: How have working conditions changed since your grandparents' generation?
Ans (OREO): From my perspective, working conditions have undergone a massive transformation, primarily due to rapid digitalization. In the past, labor was heavily physical, localized, and relationship-driven, requiring long hours of manual craft. Today, however, the workforce is highly digitized, allowing for flexible remote-working arrangements but also creating high screen fatigue. For example, my grandfather worked in physical clinics, whereas modern doctors often conduct virtual consultations. Therefore, while modern professionals enjoy greater convenience, they also face more mental stress than previous generations.

05 Describe an exciting activity you have tried for the first time

Part 2 Cue Card Bullets:

You should say: What the activity was; When and where you tried it; How you did it; And explain why you found it exciting.

Band 7.5+ Sample Response (Monologue):

"I want to share my experience of trying paragliding for the very first time. This took place last September during the annual ripening rice festival in Mu Cang Chai, which is famous for its gorgeous terraced fields. Under the direct supervision of a certified tandem pilot, I was strapped into a harness, given safety instructions, and told to run off the edge of a high cliff. Stepping off that cliff triggered an immediate, intense adrenaline rush. Within seconds, we caught a warm updraft and were gliding smoothly over the golden, cascading valleys. It was an otherworldly sensation; seeing the miniature houses, winding rivers, and vast yellow fields from a bird's-eye view was absolutely mind-blowing. I found it exciting because it forced me to break out of my comfort zone and face my fear of heights. Taking that literal leap of faith gave me an unparalleled sense of freedom and left me completely awestruck by the sheer beauty of nature."

Lexical Masterclass (Detailed Vocab & Idioms):

1. Otherworldly (adjective) /ˌʌðəˈwɜːldli/

Definition: Relating to an imaginative, beautiful, or spiritual world; strangely beautiful and peaceful.
Example: The silent underwater world of Nha Trang has an otherworldly charm.

2. Take the plunge (idiom)

Definition: To make a bold, risky, or decisive move after a period of hesitation.
Example: I hesitated for months before deciding to take the plunge and sign up for paragliding.

3. Mind-blowing (adjective)

Definition: Extremely impressive, exciting, or shocking to the mind.
Example: The panoramic view of the golden valley from a thousand meters above was absolutely mind-blowing.

Part 3 Deep Dive (Page 56):

1. Why are some people unwilling to try new things?

Ans (OREO): Primarily, individuals refuse to experiment due to a strong aversion to uncertainty. This is because stepping outside one's comfort zone causes immediate psychological discomfort. For example, many employees stay in unfulfilling jobs for years because of their fear of career instability. Therefore, humans naturally tend to maintain the status quo to minimize anxiety.

Vocab Masterclass: Status quo (noun phrase) – current situation; Aversion to uncertainty (collocation) – strong dislike of not knowing what will happen.

2. Do you think fear stops people from trying new things?

Ans (OREO): Yes, fear is undoubtedly the most paralyzing inhibitor of human action. It triggers natural evolutionary instincts designed to keep us safe by avoiding unfamiliar, potentially hazardous conditions. For instance, people often hesitate to try public speaking because they anticipate social humiliation. Consequently, internal anxieties prevent people from realizing their full cognitive potential.

Vocab Masterclass: Paralyzing (adjective) – extremely scary/immobilizing; Inhibitor (noun) – something that slows down or prevents a process.

3. Why are some people keen on doing dangerous activities?

Ans (OREO): From my perspective, thrill-seekers crave high-risk scenarios because of the hormonal reward systems in our brains. Extreme actions release a massive rush of adrenaline and dopamine, which breaks up the monotony of modern daily routines. For example, office workers often enjoy rock climbing on weekends to experience intense physical sensation. Therefore, these sports serve as active psychological outlets to break the routine of a highly regulated life.

Vocab Masterclass: Thrill-seeker (noun) – someone looking for excitement; Monotony (noun) – boring repetition.

4. Do you think that children adapt to new things more easily than adults?

Ans (OREO): Without a doubt, children exhibit far greater cognitive flexibility than adults. This is due to their neuroplasticity and the absence of fixed social expectations or deep-rooted habits. For instance, young children learn foreign languages and digital tools with ease compared to older adults. Therefore, their malleable minds are fundamentally primed for fast adjustment to environmental changes.

Vocab Masterclass: Cognitive flexibility (noun phrase) – mental ability to switch between thinking about different concepts; Malleable (adjective) – easily influenced or shaped.

5. What can people learn from doing dangerous activities?

Ans (OREO): High-risk endeavors teach individuals the invaluable quality of mental fortitude and risk assessment. By confronting fear directly, a person learns how to stay calm and analytical under high pressure. For example, mountaineers must make rapid, logical decisions about weather changes when climbing. Consequently, extreme sports build actual resilience that is highly transferable to real-world business challenges.

Vocab Masterclass: Fortitude (noun) – courage in pain or adversity; Risk assessment (noun phrase) – evaluating potential dangers.

6. What are the benefits of trying new things?

Ans (OREO): Regularly attempting novel tasks provides intense cognitive stimulation and prevents mental stagnation. This is because learning new skills builds new neural connections, keeping the mind sharp and young. For instance, picked up a musical instrument at an older age drastically lowers the risk of memory decline. Therefore, a life of exploration enhances overall happiness and brain health.

Vocab Masterclass: Cognitive stimulation (noun phrase) – activities that keep the brain active; Stagnation (noun) – state of not developing or growing.

7. How can parents encourage their children to be more adventurous?

Ans (OREO): In my view, guardians can foster a spirit of adventure by providing a supportive environment where failure is celebrated as a learning step. Rather than overprotecting their children, they should praise effort over safe perfection. For example, allowing children to build structures or climb trees builds physical confidence. Thus, giving children structured freedom helps them develop independent risk-management skills.

Vocab Masterclass: Nurture (verb) – to care for and encourage growth; Structured freedom (collocation) – allowing choice within safe guidelines.

8. Do you think social media influences people to try more exciting activities?

Ans (OREO): Absolutely, digital platforms have a monumental impact on what people do for leisure today. This is because highly edited, visual feeds of extreme sports and travel trigger a strong desire to replicate those experiences. For instance, viral videos of cliff jumping often lead to huge numbers of tourists visiting those specific spots. Therefore, online trends actively shape modern recreational behaviors.

Vocab Masterclass: Desire to replicate (collocation) – wanting to copy; Leisure habits (collocation) – how people spend free time.

9. What safety measures should be taken when doing extreme sports?

Ans (OREO): First and foremost, thrill-seekers must strictly adhere to stringent safety protocols. This involves utilizing certified equipment, performing check routines, and hiring qualified professionals. For example, skydivers must pack their secondary reserve parachutes with absolute care. Therefore, meticulous preparation is essential to enjoy extreme sports without putting lives at risk.

Vocab Masterclass: Stringent protocols (noun phrase) – strict rules; Meticulous preparation (collocation) – incredibly careful planning.

10. Is it important for schools to offer adventurous activities to students?

Ans (OREO): In my estimation, integrating physical outdoor programs into school curricula is highly beneficial. This is because standardized academic subjects cannot build key interpersonal qualities such as trust, leadership, and grit. For example, kids participating in survival camp activities learn how to coordinate roles and solve problems under stress. Therefore, adventurous training is essential for a student's holistic growth.

Vocab Masterclass: Grit (noun) – courage and resolve; strength of character; Holistic growth (noun phrase) – developing all areas of a person's life.

06 Describe a time when you told your friend an important truth

Part 2 Cue Card Bullets:

You should say: Who the friend was; What the truth was; How you broke the news to them; And explain how they reacted and how it affected your friendship.

Band 7.5+ Sample Response (Monologue):

"I'd like to talk about a difficult time when I had to tell my best friend, Huy, a bitter truth about his business partner. Huy and his partner had recently launched a small startup, but I discovered through a mutual acquaintance in accounting that his partner was secretly mismanaging their shared investments. I knew that sharing this would be a bitter pill for Huy to swallow, but I felt that true friendship demanded brutal honesty rather than comfortable silence. I invited Huy over to my apartment for a quiet dinner to break the news privately. When I showed him the financial discrepancy records, he was initially taken aback and became defensive, almost refusing to believe me. However, because we have a rock-solid, long-term friendship, he took a step back and decided to audit the books himself. While it was a painful process that ultimately led to the dissolution of his partnership, it saved him from severe financial ruin. In the end, this experience cleared the air and made our friendship even stronger."

Lexical Masterclass (Detailed Vocab & Idioms):

1. Brutal honesty (noun phrase)

Definition: Expressing the absolute, unvarnished truth even if it is uncomfortable or causes emotional pain.
Example: True friendship occasionally requires brutal honesty rather than polite flattery.

2. Bitter pill to swallow (idiom)

Definition: An unpleasant fact or situation that is difficult to accept but must be faced.
Example: Admitting that his business partner was untrustworthy was a bitter pill to swallow for Huy.

3. Clear the air (idiom)

Definition: To defuse tension, remove anger, or clarify misunderstandings by discussing them openly.
Example: Sitting down to clear the air helped the co-founders align their vision once more.

Part 3 Analytical Extension:

Q: Do you think honesty is always the best policy in a friendship?
Ans (OREO): In my view, while honesty is the cornerstone of any sustainable friendship, it must be balanced with empathy and tact. This is because absolute, blunt truth delivered without sensitivity can sometimes cause unnecessary emotional harm and fracture trust. For example, if a friend asks for feedback on a creative project they worked hard on, constructive, encouraging critique is far more helpful than harsh, unhelpful criticism. Therefore, honesty should always be the policy, but the *delivery* of that honesty must be handled with great care.

Band 7.5+ Sample Response (Monologue):

"I'd like to describe an incredibly stimulating, thought-provoking discussion I had with my classmate, Lan, on a rainy Sunday afternoon. We were sitting in a quiet, cozy coffee shop in the heart of Hanoi. Our conversation began with our current university majors but quickly sparked a debate regarding the societal impacts of Artificial Intelligence on human creativity. Lan was of the opinion that advanced generative AI models would completely replace human novelists and painters within a decade, citing their speed and vast database of knowledge. On the other hand, I strongly disagreed, arguing that art requires authentic, subjective emotional depth and life experiences that machines simply cannot replicate. It was a wonderful exercise in critical thinking that gave me a lot of food for thought long after we left the cafe."

Example: The speaker raised several thought-provoking points about ethical technology use.

2. Spark a debate (collocation)

Definition: To trigger a public or personal discussion, argument, or disagreement.
Example: The proposal to ban personal vehicles in the city center has sparked a heated debate.

3. Food for thought (idiom)

Definition: Anything that provides mental stimulus or material for reflection and contemplation.
Example: Reading the essay on global warming gave me plenty of food for thought.

Part 3 Analytical Extension:

Q: Why is it important to discuss topics with people who hold different opinions?
Ans (OREO): In my view, engaging with opposing viewpoints is crucial for cognitive development and preventing intellectual isolation. This is because discussing opposing ideas forces us to re-examine our own beliefs, challenge our biases, and build empathy. For example, debating a classmate on environmental regulations might help one understand the economic challenges faced by small businesses. Therefore, constructive discussions with diverse minds are essential for developing a well-rounded and objective worldview.

08 Describe an important decision that you made

Part 2 Cue Card Bullets:

You should say: What decision it was; Why you had to make it; What options were available; And explain why it was an important decision for you.

Band 7.5+ Sample Response (Monologue):

"The most significant, defining decision I made was leaving a stable, well-paying job in corporate banking to pursue a career in graphic design. I stood at a major crossroads in my life; my parents wanted me to play it safe and stay in banking, while I craved creative fulfillment and professional autonomy. I spent several sleepless nights weighing the pros and cons, burning the midnight oil to build a basic design portfolio. I had two options: remain in my comfortable but unexciting office job, or take a massive leap of faith into a highly competitive creative industry. Ultimately, I chose to resign. This decision shaped my life completely. It was incredibly risky, but it allowed me to align my career with my natural passions. Today, running my own successful boutique design studio, I feel genuinely fulfilled, proving that taking calculated risks is sometimes essential for personal growth."

Lexical Masterclass (Detailed Vocab & Idioms):

1. Crossroads (noun) /ˈkrɒsrəʊdz/

Definition: A point at which a crucial, life-changing decision must be made.
Example: After graduation, many students find themselves at a major career crossroads.

2. Weigh the pros and cons (idiom)

Definition: To carefully consider the advantages and disadvantages of a situation before acting.
Example: You should weigh the pros and cons before committing to an expensive loan.

3. Take a leap of faith (idiom)

Definition: To make a bold choice without knowing the outcome, trusting that things will turn out well.
Example: Relocating to a new country without a pre-arranged job was a major leap of faith.

Part 3 Deep Dive (Page 59):

1. Do you think children sometimes have to make important decisions?

Ans (OREO): Yes, although children's choices may seem minor to adults, they are deeply important for their age. Making decisions about peer groups, hobbies, or managing their study schedule builds their early sense of responsibility. For example, a child choosing to allocate time to practice piano over playing video games develops discipline. Therefore, navigating these choices during formative years lays the groundwork for adulthood autonomy.

Vocab Masterclass: Formative years (noun phrase) – early childhood period shaping future development; Autonomy (noun) – independence or freedom.

2. What important decisions do teenagers need to make after graduation?

Ans (OREO): Following high school graduation, adolescents stand at a major crossroads where they must make pivotal life decisions. They need to choose whether to pursue higher education, enter the workforce directly, or take a gap year to travel. For instance, selecting a university major determines a teenager's long-term academic and professional career path. Thus, these early choices act as primary drivers of their future livelihood.

Vocab Masterclass: Pivotal (adjective) – of crucial importance; Academic trajectory (noun phrase) – course or path of study.

3. Who can children turn to for help when making a decision?

Ans (OREO): Children should primarily seek guidance from trusted figures within their close social circles. Parents and close family members serve as the most natural advisers because they understand the child's character intimately. Additionally, schoolteachers or counselors can offer objective academic advice. For example, a student struggling to choose their school subjects will find immense clarity by discussing options with their career counselor. Therefore, having a reliable confidant helps children avoid bad choices.

Vocab Masterclass: Confidant (noun) – a person with whom one shares a secret or private matter; Seek guidance (collocation) – to ask for advice.

4. Do you think advertisements can influence our decisions when shopping?

Ans (OREO): Absolutely, marketing campaigns are highly engineered to sway consumer purchasing behavior. This is done by leveraging emotional triggers and subliminal messages to create an artificial need for a product. For instance, seeing a popular celebrity endorse a smartphone model often drives consumers to buy it on impulse. Therefore, advertising plays a massive, often invisible role in driving retail sales.

Vocab Masterclass: Subliminal messages (noun phrase) – sensory stimuli below the threshold of conscious perception; Impulse buying (noun phrase) – purchasing items without pre-planning.

5. Do you think the influence of advertising is good?

Ans (OREO): In my view, the impact of commercial advertising is a double-edged sword. On the positive side, it raises awareness about innovative products and fosters market competition, which can drive down prices. On the negative side, it cultivates a culture of extreme consumerism and makes people dissatisfied with what they have. For example, children are often manipulated by toy ads into demanding expensive gifts. Therefore, while advertising keeps the economic engine running, it requires consumers to exercise critical awareness.

Vocab Masterclass: Double-edged sword (idiom) – a situation or decision that has both positive and negative consequences; Consumerism (noun) – the preoccupation of society with the acquisition of consumer goods.

6. How do people usually make important decisions?

Ans (OREO): Generally, people approach major life choices using a mix of rational analysis and intuitive feel. This process involves listing pros and cons, consulting experts, and predicting future scenarios. For instance, when buying a house, a family will calculate budgets and mortgage rates, while also relying on their gut instinct about the neighborhood. Therefore, successful decision-making is fundamentally a balance between cold facts and subjective intuition.

Vocab Masterclass: Rational analysis (noun phrase) – logical evaluation based on data; Gut instinct (idiom) – immediate intuitive feel or reaction.
