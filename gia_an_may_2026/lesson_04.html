90-Minute Writing Masterclass (Environment Focus)

Writing (90 Min)

1. Writing Task 2 Instruction

A. Deconstructing Prompts & Brainstorming

1. Analyzing the Prompt (The 3-Step Scan): Teach the student to instantly identify three things before writing a single word:

  • Topic Words: The broad subject (e.g., Environment, Education).
  • Micro-Keywords: The specific angle (e.g., plastic packaging, not just "pollution"). Missing this causes off-topic penalties.
  • Instruction Words: What is the task? (e.g., To what extent do you agree? vs Discuss both views).

2. Brainstorming (The 'Why/How' Method): Instead of starring at a blank page, teach them to ask "Why?" and "What is the result?" of the statement. Generate exactly 2 strong ideas—no more, no less. Quality over quantity.

B. Mastering the 4-Paragraph Structure

Enforce strict adherence to the 4-paragraph model for standard Opinion essays to guarantee optimal Coherence & Cohesion (CC) scoring:

1. Introduction (2-3 sentences)
  • • General Background / Hook
  • • Paraphrase the prompt
  • • Thesis Statement
2. Body Paragraph 1 (4-5 sentences)
  • • Topic Sentence (Idea 1)
  • • Explanation (Why/How?)
  • • Example / Consequence
3. Body Paragraph 2 (4-5 sentences)
  • • Topic Sentence (Idea 2)
  • • Explanation (Why/How?)
  • • Example / Consequence
4. Conclusion (1-2 sentences)
  • • Restate Thesis (different words)
  • • Final summary thought (No new ideas)

C. Formulating Strong Thesis Statements

The Golden Rule: The examiner must know exactly what the student's position is by the end of the introduction. No "fence-sitting".

Formula for "To what extent do you agree?":

"While there are valid arguments to the contrary, I [completely/mostly] [agree/disagree] that [paraphrased opinion] because [brief mention of Reason 1] and [brief mention of Reason 2]."

Tutor Tip: Remind the student never to use phrases like "In this essay I will discuss..." — it wastes words and lowers the stylistic tone.

D. Discussion Essay Phrase Bank & Cohesive Lexis

Equip the student with advanced, high-scoring transitional structures to balance and pivot opposing viewpoints (Band 7.5+ Target):

Presenting View A (Objective Grounding)
  • "Advocates of this standpoint argue that..."
  • "The primary justification supporting this school of thought is..."
  • "A compelling argument in favor of [X] centers on..."
  • "Proponents maintain that [Y] yields far-reaching benefits..."
Pivoting to View B (Opposing Counter-Stance)
  • "On the flip side, a substantial body of opinion holds that..."
  • "Opponents of this perspective, however, counter that..."
  • "Nonetheless, there are highly persuasive grounds to suggest..."
  • "Despite the validity of the aforementioned claims, detractors..."
Synthesizing Personal Stance (Thesis & Transitions)

Thesis Synthesizers:

  • "While acknowledging the merit of both arguments, I firmly believe..."
  • "On balance, it is my conviction that [X] holds greater weight because..."
  • "While compelling arguments can be marshaled on both sides, my position..."

Cohesive Adverbials:

  • "Conversely,..." / "On the contrary,..."
  • "That being said,..." / "Be that as it may,..."
  • "By stark contrast,..." / "In sharp contradiction..."

2. Task 2 Practice: Environment

Live prompt breakdown and structuring

Environment Vocab List

Work through these real-test environment prompts with the student. Focus on brainstorming the ideas and forming the structure rather than writing the full essay.

1

"Some people believe that the best way to solve environmental problems is to increase the price of fuel. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"

Brainstorming Direction: Disagree (Mostly)

While it might reduce driving slightly, it heavily burdens lower-income individuals and ignores industrial pollution, which is a larger factor. Better alternatives exist.

Essay Structure
  • Body 1 (Concession): Why higher prices might initially help (Deters unnecessary driving, encourages public transport).
  • Body 2 (Main Argument): Why it fails as the "best" way (Disproportionately affects the working class, increases cost of goods/logistics, ignores corporate emissions).
  • Alternative Solutions: Investing heavily in renewable energy infrastructure and enforcing strict industrial regulations.
2

"The increase in the production of consumer goods results in damage to the natural environment. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"

Brainstorming Direction: Completely Agree

The lifecycle of fast-moving consumer goods (from raw material extraction to final disposal) is inherently destructive to ecosystems.

Essay Structure
  • Body 1 (The Production Phase): Extraction of natural resources (deforestation for packaging, mining for electronics), massive energy consumption, and factory emissions.
  • Body 2 (The Disposal Phase): The rise of "throwaway culture". Products are designed with short lifespans (planned obsolescence), leading to overflowing landfills and ocean plastic pollution.
3

"Some people think that environmental problems are too big for individuals to solve, and it is entirely the responsibility of the government. To what extent do you agree or disagree?"

Brainstorming Direction: Disagree (Shared Responsibility)

While governments have the power to enact macro-level changes, individual grassroots actions collectively drive market shifts and force political action.

Essay Structure
  • Body 1 (Government Role): Acknowledge why people think this. Governments can pass laws (carbon taxes, banning single-use plastics) and fund large-scale renewable projects.
  • Body 2 (Individual Power): Why individuals are still crucial. Consumer choices dictate corporate behavior (boycotting unethical brands). Micro-actions (recycling, reducing meat consumption) multiply globally to have massive impacts.
