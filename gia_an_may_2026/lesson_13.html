1. Reading: Matching Information

Locating specific arguments & detailed ideas across paragraphs

Matching Info Strategy: Scanning for Functional Outlines

Unlike Matching Headings (which looks for global main ideas of paragraphs), Matching Information requires finding a highly specific, localized piece of detail hidden within a paragraph. Questions often appear as: "Which paragraph contains... a reference to, an explanation of, a comparison between..."

Execution Methodologies (Tackling Steps):

1. Understand the Functional Prompts: Teach the student to recognize the "type" of information needed:

  • "a definition of..." → Look for copular verbs, brackets, or relative clauses (e.g., "referred to as", "which is defined as").
  • "a comparison..." → Scan for comparative structures, comparative adjectives, or words like "unlike", "similar to", "balanced against".
  • "numerical evidence..." → Target statistics, percentages, years, ratios.

2. Skim, Don't Translate: The statements are always heavily paraphrased. Tell the student to read the statements first, circle the key concepts, and then skim the paragraphs to search for contextual synonyms rather than literal matching words.

3. Non-Sequential Order Warning: Remind the student that this question type is **not** in chronological order. Tell them to do this task *after* other sequential tasks (like Gap-filling or T/F/NG) so they already have some familiarity with the passage layout.

2. Speaking Part 2: Narrative Storytelling

Strategy: Narrative Tenses for Dynamic Monologues

To reach Band 6.5+ in grammatical range and coherence, students must move beyond repetitive past simple structures ("I went there", "I saw that"). Enforce standard sequencing rules utilizing complex narrative tenses:

  • Past Simple (Main Action): To describe sequential completed actions. ("We hiked up to the viewpoint.")
  • Past Continuous (Background Setting): To build atmospheres or describe temporary parallel events. ("While we were driving up, the mist was rolling in.")
  • Past Perfect (Retrospective Setup): To introduce background experiences preceding the main story. ("Before that trip, I had never witnessed a real waterfall.")
