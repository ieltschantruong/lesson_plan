2. Reading Section

Y/N/NG Strategy: Fact vs. Writer's Claim

While True/False/Not Given deals with verified objective facts, Yes/No/Not Given explicitly targets the writer’s opinion, claim, or attitude. This is highly common in Passage 3.

Key Tackling Methodologies:

1. Opinion-Signaling Verbs: Train the student to scan the passage for subjective triggers: claims, believes, suggests, argues, fears, suspects.

2. Analyzing Qualifiers: Watch out for words that soften or strengthen a claim: possibly, undoubtedly, presumably, minor, significant. A question with an absolute qualifier like "completely" is often NO if the text uses a softer variant.

3. Guard against Outside Knowledge: Passage 3 topics (like medicine or psychology) often trigger personal biases. Emphasize that if the writer does not make the specific claim, it is NOT GIVEN, even if scientifically true.

3. Listening Section

Listening P4: Navigating Undivided Academic Lectures

Section 4 Structure: A continuous, 10-question monologue of a university lecture. Unlike other parts, there is no break in the middle of the audio to read upcoming questions.

CBT Execution Framework:

• Exhaustion Prep (Sustained Focus): Since the lecture runs non-stop, train the student to scan all 10 blanks in the initial 45 seconds of preparation. Identify word classes (noun, verb, adjective) and prepositions beforehand.

• Track Signposting Vocabulary: Lectures are organized logically. Teach the student to listen for transition landmarks to avoid getting lost: "Moving on to...", "If we examine the historical records...", "Let's turn our attention to..."

• Spelling in Context: Section 4 vocabulary is academic. Ensure the student practices rapid short-hand typing on screen during the test and double-checks spelling during the final review phase.
