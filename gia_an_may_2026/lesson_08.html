2. Listening Section

Listening P3: Identifying Self-Correction & Matching Info

1. Speaker Self-Correction Patterns: In Part 3 academic discussions, speakers often state an initial choice and immediately revise it to trick passive listeners.

The Trap Structure: Speaker A proposes Option X → Speaker B questions it → Speaker A corrects themselves with Option Y.

Self-Correction Signals: Train the student to register transition words like: "On second thought...", "Actually, let's change that...", "No, wait, I made a mistake...", "I used to think so, but instead..."

2. Matching Information Strategies (CBT Focus):

  • Paraphrase Mapping: The numbered list in matching tasks (e.g., specific ideas/topics) remains static, but the options are heavily paraphrased in the audio. Focus on synonyms rather than direct keywords.
  • Screen Navigation: On the Computer-Based Test (CBT), matching exercises usually use drag-and-drop or select menus. Ensure the student practices looking back and forth from the static list to the moving options without losing focus.
  • Chronological Rule: The numbered items will always be heard in order. The lettered options are scattered. Keep your cursor hovering near the relevant list element.

3. Speaking Section

Speaking Part 3 Execution: Utilizing the A.R.E.A Framework

Connecting Part 2 Storytelling to Part 3 Abstract Logic: Help the student shift away from concrete personal stories (Part 2) to broader societal and objective viewpoints (Part 3).

The A.R.E.A Structure
A — Answer Answer the question directly using synonyms from the prompt. Avoid "Yes" or "No" answers.
R — Reason Explain *why* you hold this view. Introduce connecting words like "due to the fact that..." or "primarily because...".
E — Example Provide a general or social example (avoid personal "I" stories if possible; use "In major cities..." or "According to surveys...").
A — Alternative Wrap up by presenting a counter-perspective or conditional outcome: "Otherwise, we might see..." or "Unless this is resolved..."
